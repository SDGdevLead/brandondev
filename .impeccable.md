# .impeccable.md

## Design Context

### Users
Primary audience: hiring managers at product companies. They arrive with intent — someone sent them this link, or they clicked from a resume — and they have 90 seconds before they decide whether to keep reading. They are scanning for taste, craft, and evidence of real work. Secondary audience: other developers and designers who may refer Brandon onward.

### Brand Personality
**3-word voice: human, colourful, precise.**

Brandon has an unusual background — psychology + front-end development + design — and the portfolio should reflect that synthesis rather than hide it. "Human" from the psychology lens and the warmth of the overall aesthetic. "Colourful" because the palette is genuinely vibrant (coral, teal, warm neutrals — all derived from the flower photograph used in the hero). "Precise" because the code and design are both deliberately crafted, not assembled from templates.

Emotional goal: a hiring manager should feel that the person who built this has taste, cares about details, and would raise the quality of any team they joined.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Bold and editorial — not minimal, not maximalist.**

Reference site: kimbrandesign.com. The appeal is not the neutral palette (Brandon's is the opposite — vibrant) but the editorial confidence: work is shown with intention, layouts feel considered, there's a sense of curation rather than just content-dumping.

Anti-reference: generic developer portfolio — dark mode, neon accent, identical card grid with icons, hero that just says "Hi, I'm a developer." If you'd mistake it for a template, it's wrong.

**Theme:** Light. The flower hero is the entry point and it sets a warm, organic mood. Dark mode would fight the coral/teal palette and undercut the human quality.

**Colour palette:** Derived from the flower photograph. Coral primary (oklch ~65% 0.185 42), teal accent (oklch ~68% 0.115 196), warm beige neutrals. The palette is already good — honour it.

**Hero:** The circular clip-path reveal with mix-blend-mode portrait is the most distinctive thing in the design. It stays. It's the thing someone will remember and describe to a colleague.

### Design Principles

1. **Make taste legible in 90 seconds.** Every above-the-fold decision should communicate that the person who built this has an eye. Hiring managers don't read — they pattern-match.

2. **The colour is a feature, not decoration.** The palette comes from the flower photo and is the identity of the site. Don't dilute it with safe neutrals — let the coral and teal do real work.

3. **Show, don't describe.** The portfolio sections should demonstrate skill through the quality of their own design, not through lists of credentials. Real project thumbnails beat placeholder boxes.

4. **Editorial over template.** Layouts should feel considered and slightly unexpected — asymmetric where symmetry would be obvious, varied spacing rather than uniform padding, one layout choice per section that couldn't come from a theme.

5. **Human first.** The psychology background is a differentiator. The copy and structure should reflect that this is someone who thinks about people, not just pixels.
